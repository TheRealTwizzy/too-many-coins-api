# Too Many Coins — Descriptive Unified Design Summary

## Source Basis

This document consolidates the uploaded unified summary, the uploaded markdown summary file, the table of contents, and Chapters 01 through 15 into one descriptive markdown reference. It is written as a compact design brief rather than a chapter-by-chapter restatement. Where multiple chapters define the same idea from different angles, this file keeps the canonical meaning once and folds supporting details into the most useful place.

## Executive Overview

Too Many Coins is a deterministic, season-based economic competition built around constrained scarcity, transparent rules, and strategic sacrifice. The game runs on overlapping 28-day seasons that begin every 7 days, which means four active seasons normally exist at once. A player may spectate any season, but may economically participate in only one active season at a time. Each season is a closed economy. Coins are created only by Universal Basic Income, spent to buy Seasonal Stars, and never minted by trades, grants, staff tools, or side systems. Seasonal Stars function as both rank and spendable power budget, because they determine seasonal leaderboard position while also funding purchases from the seasonal Sigil Vault. Sigils create temporary leverage inside the season, but they do not persist. Remaining Seasonal Stars convert one-to-one into Global Stars when a player successfully Lock-Ins or when the season ends naturally for an eligible end-finisher. Global Stars accumulate only within the current yearly cycle, drive yearly standing, can be spent only on cosmetics, and are wiped at yearly reset.

The game’s emotional center is not accumulation without consequence. It is tension between present strength and preserved status. A player earns Coins, converts Coins into Seasonal Stars, decides whether to keep those Stars for rank or spend them for tactical advantage, competes under fixed timing rules, and eventually chooses between safety and upside. Lock-In protects current value by converting present Seasonal Stars into Global Stars before season end, but it gives up natural-end bonuses. Staying to the end keeps the player in contention for participation and placement bonuses, but it also exposes them to the season’s remaining risk. That pressure is the intended fantasy. The design is built so outcomes emerge from timing, opportunity, scarcity, and deliberate trade-offs rather than from hidden systems, permanent power growth, or administrative discretion.

## Core Canon and Design Doctrine

The entire game is governed by a few non-negotiable principles. Server authority is absolute over time, legality, ordering, balances, quotes, rankings, and outcomes. The simulation runs on a single global one-second tick cadence. Global timing uses `global_tick_index`, which is identical to authoritative server time in integer Unix seconds. Season-local timing uses `season_tick_index`, derived from the season’s fixed start time. Determinism is mandatory. Ordering, math, random draws, idempotency handling, and persistence behavior must replay identically from authoritative inputs. Immutable logs and snapshot chains exist because the game is intended to be auditable, not merely functional. If a rule is not explicitly defined in canon, it does not exist. That applies equally to mechanics, social affordances, moderation authority, persistence, and UI assumptions.

The doctrine also sharply limits what the game is not. Social systems must never transfer value, count as gameplay activity, or modify economy state. Staff cannot edit outcomes, economy values, timing, placements, rewards, cosmetics, or competitive history. Profile deletion exists, but account deletion does not; deletion is irreversible redaction of player-facing identity rather than erasure of authoritative history. Official system framing also refuses punitive language around ordinary outcomes. A player who Lock-Ins early, finishes with little value, idles, re-enters, or simply participates without placing is not considered a loser by system doctrine. The design wants pressure and consequence, but not shame-based rhetoric.

## Game Concept, Audience, and Intent

The game concept is a competitive economy where value enters slowly, scarcity is real, and every resource choice carries opportunity cost. A player is meant to feel the constant pull between accumulation and spending, security and ambition, and immediate leverage versus preserved status. Seasonal Stars embody that tension more clearly than any other resource. Holding them protects leaderboard strength, while spending them on Sigils can improve present tactical position at the cost of rank. Lock-In adds another layer by allowing the player to step off the field and secure current value as Global Stars, trading away the possibility of natural-end bonuses and late-season movement.

The design goals emphasize structural fairness, clarity, and strategic timing rather than constant attendance. Players should not need to live in the game every hour to compete. Advantage is meant to come from choosing moments well, understanding deterministic surfaces, responding to scarcity, and accepting calculated risk. Hidden progression, tenure-based soft power, vague exceptions, and administrative interventions are all excluded because they would dilute that clarity. The target audience is therefore players who enjoy transparent competition, long-form planning, economic trade-offs, and prestige earned within rigid systems rather than through grind ladders or monetized acceleration.

## Seasonal Structure and High-Level Flow

Seasons are fixed-duration, fixed-cadence economic containers. Every season lasts exactly 28 days and begins exactly 7 days after the previous season’s start, anchored to a permanent universal schedule. In normal operation this creates four active seasons at once. A player may freely browse scheduled, active, and ended seasons as a spectator, but only one active season may be their participation season at any given time. Joining a season, leaving it through Lock-In, or being finalized at natural end all carry authoritative state transitions defined by server timing, not by the client.

The high-level gameplay loop is straightforward. A player joins or re-enters an active season, begins accruing Coins through UBI while participating, converts Coins into Seasonal Stars at server-published prices, optionally spends Seasonal Stars on Sigils from a finite shared vault, uses available mechanics to pursue a better seasonal position, and eventually exits the season either by Lock-In or natural end. On exit, current Seasonal Stars convert to Global Stars one-to-one under the appropriate rules. Natural end may add participation and placement bonuses in Global Stars for eligible end-finishers. Across the year, the player accumulates Global Stars, may spend some of them on cosmetics, competes on the yearly leaderboard, and eventually hits a yearly reset that clears Global Stars and yearly standings while preserving badges, cosmetics, and required historical records.

## Activity, Participation, and Re-Entry

The participation model is intentionally narrow. A player is either economically participating in a season or not. Activity state inside participation is a separate closed set of `Active` and `Idle`. Connectivity is tracked independently, so disconnect is not treated as a gameplay activity state. Idle begins after 900 seconds, or 15 minutes, without a qualifying gameplay decision while participating. Being Idle matters because it changes UBI behavior and blocks season-scoped economy actions, but it does not block chat. A disconnected player is not assumed to have made a decision; disconnect is transport loss only and never implies voluntary intent.

Re-entry is allowed into the same active season after exit, but it is not a continuation of prior seasonal power. Re-entry creates a fresh season-bound resource state with no preserved Coins, Seasonal Stars, Sigils, boosts, or leverage. The only meaningful continuity is participation time history used later for post-season participation bonus calculations. Re-entry therefore preserves evaluative history without preserving mechanical advantage. This reinforces the project’s insistence that time spent in the system is not itself a progression ladder.

## Authoritative Tick Architecture and Action Processing

The game’s authoritative simulation advances on a global one-second tick. At each tick, the server follows a fixed order: classify the tick and capture the authoritative start-of-tick snapshot, process scheduled system events, run Sigil drop evaluation, resolve player actions, execute system math and UBI accrual, publish the next authoritative surface for prices and other derived values, evaluate activity transitions, recalculate leaderboards, and persist audit and state records. This order is not descriptive flavor. It is the reason deterministic replay is possible. Any subsystem that needs snapshot inputs, legality checks, quotes, or timing must fit this order exactly.

Requests that arrive after the commit cutoff for a tick do not slide into that tick opportunistically; they are buffered to the next intake boundary. Each player may have only one unresolved non-exempt state-changing action at a time, and trades occupy that pending slot for both parties involved. Accepted actions receive authoritative sequencing through `accept_seq`, lock their required economic surface inputs at acceptance, and carry explicit resolution and effect timing. Cross-scope action ordering is also fixed. If a player has both season-scoped and global actions pending at the same commit boundary, season actions resolve before global actions. This matters, for example, when a Lock-In and a cosmetic purchase both exist around the same boundary, because the Lock-In conversion to Global Stars must happen first before any cosmetic spend checks the updated balance.

The allowed season action set is closed. It includes season join, purchase of Seasonal Stars, purchase from the Sigil Vault, boost purchase, trade actions, Lock-In confirmation, idle acknowledgment, and season-end acknowledgment. If an action is not in canon, it does not exist. Defensive invalidation is also tightly constrained. Accepted actions are not later canceled simply because conditions changed in a fuzzy way; invalidation causes are closed and replay-safe.

## Sigil Drops, Sigil Vault, and Temporary Power

Sigils are the game’s season-bound strategic resources. They are not progression gates, and they are never persistent. A player may acquire them either through deterministic probabilistic drops while actively participating or by spending Seasonal Stars in the shared seasonal Sigil Vault. Their entire function is temporary leverage within the current season, whether through trading or by spending them on boosts. When a player exits the season or when the season expires, Sigils are destroyed with the rest of live seasonal economy state.

Sigil drops are intentionally constrained. A player is eligible for drop evaluation on a tick only if the authoritative start-of-tick snapshot says the player is online, participating, and Active. The drop system allows at most one Sigil per eligible tick and is isolated from UBI, boosts, and system-event influence. The base drop chance is one success trial per eligible player per tick at a rate of one in fifty thousand, with deterministic tier odds heavily weighted toward lower tiers. The system also includes a pity rule that forces a Tier I drop after 120,000 eligible ticks without a drop, and a throttle that caps a player at three Sigil drops in any rolling 86,400-tick window. Drop evaluation is suppressed on the last valid economic tick and on the expiration tick. It may also be suppressed by an operational safety latch that becomes effective only at the next tick boundary and is controlled as an audited operational flag rather than as a staff gameplay action.

The Sigil Vault adds planned scarcity. Vault inventory is finite, globally shared within the season, and non-replenishing. Tiers can be effectively disabled by zero configured supply, in which case they cannot appear, be purchased, or be traded. Purchasing a Sigil from the vault destroys Seasonal Stars immediately. Vault costs are exposed through authoritative surfaces and change with remaining inventory according to chapter-owned pricing tables. This means Sigils can enter circulation through both random drops and deliberate purchases, but always under deterministic inventory and pricing contracts.

## Economy Model, UBI, and Pricing

The economy is intentionally narrow and closed. Coins are the only live seasonal currency and are minted only by UBI. There are no tutorial grants, admin grants, conversions from external sources, trade minting, or hidden faucets. Coins exist only inside a season, are visible only to the owning player in the participation season, and are destroyed by Seasonal Star purchases, trade fees, Lock-In cleanup, and season expiration. Seasonal Stars are created only by burning Coins at the authoritative star price. They are then used either as the player’s live seasonal ranking value or spent on the Sigil Vault. Global Stars are created only by one-to-one conversion of Seasonal Stars and by natural-end bonuses. They are non-tradable, globally visible as status, and spendable only on cosmetics. Cosmetics are the only meta unlock path, and they have zero mechanical effect.

Economy math uses fixed-point rules, floor-after-each-step arithmetic, and wide intermediates with overflow rejection. Per-season economy configuration is immutable once a season begins. The server exposes two authoritative economic surfaces: the internal `math_surface(T)` used for tick-T UBI math and related calculations, and the published surface used for next-tick acceptance-time quotes such as StarPrice and vault costs. This distinction matters because UBI uses post-action, same-tick state while acceptance-time purchases and trades use the previously published surface that was finalized at the end of the prior tick.

UBI is the only Coin faucet, but it is not flat or naive. It depends on activity branch, inflation dampening, hoarding suppression, and modifier sums, all within fixed clamps. If a player Lock-Ins on a tick, same-tick UBI is suppressed to zero for that player. Join timing is explicit so that new participation becomes economically effective only when the relevant snapshot says it is. Inflation dampening is based on total seasonal Coin supply. Hoarding suppression looks at recent spend behavior and can reduce UBI when a player stops circulating value, but it never boosts beyond canonical constraints. The result is a system that slowly injects value while pressuring players to make actual decisions rather than passively accumulate indefinitely.

StarPrice is also deterministic. It is published from end-of-tick supply and locked at action acceptance. When a player requests to buy Seasonal Stars, the request means “spend up to this many Coins,” not “force this exact Coin spend regardless of output.” The server reads the locked star price from the acceptance-time surface, computes `stars_out` as floor of requested Coins divided by price, rejects the purchase if less than one star would be received, and reserves only the actual Coins spent rather than the entire requested amount. No repricing is allowed after acceptance.

## Trading, Escrow, Valuation, and Friction

Trading exists as a deterministic exchange of Coins and Sigils only. Seasonal Stars and Global Stars are never tradeable. Coins-for-Coins trades do not exist, and one-sided gifts do not exist. Both parties must contribute positive value, and a trade’s identity is fixed by its `(season_id, trade_id)` pair. A trade begins when `trade_initiate` is accepted. At that moment, the server locks the relevant surface version, computes and stores the trade’s declared value in Coins, computes and locks the symmetric fee both parties must pay, and escrows the initiator’s offered assets plus initiator fee. The offer then becomes open. When `trade_accept` is accepted, the acceptor’s assets and fee are escrowed under the already-locked quote. If anything fails on the acceptor side, the accept is rejected with no side effects, and the initiator’s open escrow remains intact. If acceptance succeeds, the swap and fee burn happen atomically.

Trade valuation is deterministic and grounded in published market information. Sigils are valued in Coins using their vault cost in Seasonal Stars multiplied by the authoritative star price. If a vault tier is sold out on the relevant surface version, valuation falls back to the last published non-null vault cost for that tier. Fees are then derived from the trade’s total declared Coin value using tiered fee rates, subject to a minimum fee, and burned from both parties symmetrically. Trades time out 3,600 seconds after initiate acceptance. If blackout begins or the expiration tick is reached before a non-terminal trade resolves, the trade is invalidated and all locks are released. When multiple terminal transitions compete for the same trade identity, the first applied terminal transition wins and later competing transitions are rejected as replaced conflicts. The system is designed to prevent repricing, double application, and state ambiguity.

## Progression, Rank, and Prestige

Progression in Too Many Coins is descriptive rather than mechanical. In-season progression is nothing but Seasonal Stars and seasonal leaderboard position. There are no levels, XP systems, skill trees, stat growth, tenure bonuses, soft unlocks, or action-capacity growth. Sigil tiers are not progression gates; they are rarity and power classes available to any participating player who can legally acquire them. Participation time does not unlock anything during live play and is not even shown during the season. Re-entry does not preserve seasonal power and does not create continuity advantage.

Seasonal ranking is strictly ordinal. The ordering tuple is Seasonal Stars descending, then a deterministic season tie-break key ascending, then player ID ascending only as collision hardening. The tie-break key is derived from an HMAC over season seed and player identity so it is stable within the season, replay-safe, and immune to client manipulation. The authoritative seasonal ranking value is simply current Seasonal Stars. No weighting, historical carryover, or rating system exists behind the scenes.

Cross-season progression is an equally closed set. Only Global Stars within the current year, cosmetics, seasonal top-three badges, and yearly top-ten badges persist. Global Stars are the sole numeric carryover, and even they are temporary because yearly reset erases them. That makes the prestige structure deliberately brittle. A player can accumulate status across seasons, spend it down on cosmetics, lose ranking ground, and then see the numeric layer wiped on the next yearly boundary. The system preserves recognition without allowing indefinite entrenched power.

## Natural-End Awards, Lock-In, and Outcome Representation

Lock-In is the main voluntary exit mechanism. It is legal only before blackout, requires eligibility, records the latest authoritative snapshot for player-season history, converts current Seasonal Stars one-to-one into Global Stars, and destroys the player’s live season economy state. Multiple Lock-Ins can occur before cutoff in the sense that each successful Lock-In converts current Seasonal Stars and leaves the player eligible to later re-enter, but double credit is prevented because Seasonal Stars are zeroed after each conversion. Lock-In is therefore not merely a menu button. It is a competitive decision to freeze value and leave the natural-end race for bonuses.

Natural season end is stricter. The expiration tick overrides normal simulation: no drops, no UBI, no new season actions, and only deterministic finalization. Final ranks are frozen from committed state at tick `E−1`, not recomputed opportunistically after the end. Natural-end rewards are then applied in a precise order. First, final ordering is computed over the authoritative final rank entries. If the top ranked value is zero, there is no winner for badge or placement purposes, and those rewards are skipped. Participation bonus can still be granted to eligible end-finishers. Seasonal top-three badges are awarded from final ordering, subject to the zero-value guard. Placement bonuses of 100, 60, and 40 Global Stars are awarded to the top three end-finishers only. Participation bonus is then awarded to end-finishers based on Active time, computed as the floor of active ticks divided by 3,600 and capped at 56. Only after bonuses are applied do eligible end-finishers convert remaining Seasonal Stars one-to-one into Global Stars.

Outcome representation follows the same careful boundaries. A season win means final natural-end first place. Second and third place are runner-up and third place. A year win means first place on the yearly global leaderboard at the moment yearly reset executes. “Current leader” is only a snapshot description and never a promise of final result. There is no formal loss state, elimination, or game-over label. Valid success includes ordinary participation, early Lock-In, strategic spending, low-value finish, re-entry, and podium placement. The system deliberately avoids moralizing ordinary strategic variance.

## Time Model, Schedule, and Pacing

Time is fully authoritative and represented as UTC epoch seconds. The permanent season schedule is anchored to `1970-01-05T00:00:00Z`, with 28-day duration and new starts every 7 days at midnight UTC. `global_tick_index` is literally authoritative server time in integer seconds. `season_tick_start(S,t)` equals the fixed season start time plus the local tick offset. These definitions remove ambiguity from countdowns, legality windows, audit fields, and replay logic.

Pacing derives from this fixed schedule. Normal operation has four overlapping active seasons, which allows players to inspect multiple live competitions while still participating in only one. Blackout begins exactly 72 hours before season expiration and lasts through tick `E−1`. The last valid economic tick is therefore `E−1`, and the expiration tick `E` is reserved for deterministic finalization. Join and re-entry are allowed during blackout only if they become economically effective before expiration. Lock-In, by contrast, is legal only before blackout; there is no grace period into the final 72 hours. Spectatorship remains allowed across scheduled, active, and ended seasons. The server must expose authoritative countdown and urgency fields, while the client may only interpolate display between those authoritative updates. No admin timing override exists.

## Social Systems, Identity, and Spectatorship

Social systems exist, but they are explicitly non-mechanical. They never move value, trigger gameplay actions, count as participation activity, or mutate economy state. The core identity primitives are immutable `player_id`, current `handle`, optional staff-controlled `profile.display_name_override`, player-controlled profile visibility, and profile deletion state. Historical social rows and leaderboard rows store frozen `handle_snapshot` at creation time so that later handle changes do not rewrite history. Identity rendering follows a strict order: `[Removed]` for profile-deleted accounts, then display override if present, otherwise the stored snapshot.

The social graph is intentionally simple. Friendships are bidirectional and arise only through friend request and acceptance. Blocks are one-way edges, but they dominate the relationship model. If either player blocks the other, friendship is removed, pending friend requests are canceled, direct messages are disallowed, and profile viewing between the two is denied regardless of visibility setting. Profile visibility itself is a closed set of `PUBLIC`, `FRIENDS_ONLY`, and `HIDDEN`, with staff able to bypass only through moderation surfaces rather than ordinary browsing.

Handle changes are player-initiated global actions subject to strict validation. Handles are three to sixteen characters, ASCII letters digits and underscore only, case-insensitive for uniqueness, globally unique, permanently non-reusable, and blocked from reserved words. Successful changes trigger a 30-day cooldown. Historical social rows continue using their stored snapshot, so attribution remains frozen.

Communication runs through a single logical `chat_send` envelope with channel kinds for Global, Season, and DM. Chat is processed in real time, not on the season tick queue, and it does not consume the one-pending-action slot. Rejections are allowed only from a closed set of auth, ServerMode, enforcement, and channel-specific eligibility gates. DMs are one-to-one only, retained for 30 days, limited by rate caps, and must reject with a generic `dm_unavailable` reason if blocked or staff-disabled. Global chat is available to logged-in users, stores exactly the last 200 player-visible rows including moderation tombstones, and allows labeled admin posts. Season chat is available only to current participants in that season, persists only through the end of the expiration tick, and is then removed from player-visible retrieval once the tick completes. Spectators may view leaderboards but may not access season chat. No guilds, teams, parties, or pooled power structures exist.

## User Interface and Client-State Assumptions

The client is important, but it is never authoritative. UI state always distinguishes between viewing context and participation context, because a player can watch one season while actively participating in another. The interface must surface both contexts when they differ, rather than pretending the viewed season and the participation season are the same. Global Stars should always be visible, Seasonal Stars should be visible in season view, and Coins should be visible only to the owning player in the participation season. The `T+` shorthand for extremely large numbers is display-only and has no logical meaning.

Constraint communication is intentionally compact. The header chip strip is capped at two chips: one for participation mode and one for the most relevant constraint. Constraint precedence gives `Disconnected` priority over `Idle`. The disconnect modal blocks all interaction behind it. The idle modal blocks season economy actions, but chat must remain available. If the idle modal is visible while the user is viewing another season, the season chat tab still binds to the participation season so the player retains access to their real active communication context.

Economy action controls require authoritative gating fields from the server. Buttons are not allowed to infer legality locally. The server must provide the relevant `can_X` and `cannot_X_reason` flags for economy actions, while chat is exempt from that exact gating schema because it has its own real-time routing rules. Local persistence is limited to hints such as viewed season, chat tab state, and accessibility preferences. On load, reload, reconnect, or multi-tab drift, the client must fetch authoritative server state and reconcile these local hints back to reality rather than trying to restore a potentially stale competitive state from local storage.

## Failure States, Server Modes, and Recovery

Failure handling is designed to stay deterministic even when things go wrong. Disconnect means only that transport was lost; it does not imply cancelation, surrender, or acknowledgment. Every rejected action must return a closed Chapter 15 `reason_code`. Failure classes visible in the UI are just grouping labels, not alternate authoritative meanings. Server behavior under degraded conditions is bounded by a closed `ServerMode` set that includes `NORMAL`, `MAINTENANCE_LOCKDOWN`, `READ_ONLY_ECONOMY`, `LOCKDOWN_CONNECTIONS`, and `RATE_LIMIT_ACTIONS`. Any mode change becomes effective only on the next tick boundary, and actions already accepted under prior legality snapshots must settle under those snapshots rather than being retroactively reinterpreted.

Idle handling continues to follow the backend contract under these conditions. Idle persists across viewing changes, reconnects, and global surface updates. It blocks season economy actions only. Chat remains exempt. Accepted actions are not undone simply because the player later enters Idle before they resolve. Expiration finalization must be safe to retry and externally exactly once. Post-expiration acknowledgments are idempotent and may collapse into deterministic no-op success after retention compaction. Duplicate requests in general must be recognized and handled idempotently rather than applied twice. After a rejection, the client is required to refresh authoritative snapshot state.

## Moderation, Staff Authority, and Governance

Governance is intentionally narrow, because staff power is treated as a threat to competitive legitimacy if left vague. Roles are a closed set of Player, Moderator, and Admin. Moderator and Admin accounts are separate identities and are hard-blocked from season participation and economy actions. Staff cannot alter balances, inventories, boosts, prices, ranks, rewards, timing, badges, or cosmetics. They exist only for social safety, policy enforcement, reporting, evidence review, profile compliance, and related governance surfaces.

Admins have broader powers than Moderators, but even those powers are constrained. Admins can manage cases, moderation actions, profile enforcement fields, profile redaction, bans, evidence access, and audit visibility. Moderators can moderate messages, warnings, mutes, reports, and case escalation, but cannot ban or delete profiles. High-impact actions require re-authentication with 2FA, a mandatory reason code, audit logging, user notification, and cooldown or rate-limiting safeguards. Profile force-edit capability is limited to a specific allowlist of enforcement-relevant profile fields. Data correction is never done by manually editing a specific player’s economy or outcome; remediation must happen through deterministic code-driven re-derivation.

Profile deletion is admin-only and means redaction, not erasure. It is irreversible and does not change rankings, awards, ledgers, message ordering, or historical fact storage. Moderated message removal is a soft-delete tombstone that preserves position in chat ordering by replacing content in place with a removal marker. Removed and reported messages are also copied into evidence storage along with contextual prior messages for staff review. Mutes block sending only and can apply across Global, Season, and DM contexts. Reporting supports message and user reports, duplicate merging, anti-abuse limits, privacy-protected handling, and mandatory case linkage. The doctrine of non-intervention forbids hidden corrective edits to competitive outcomes and forbids using staff power to influence the economy or private advantage. There is explicitly no undefined break-glass power that overrides this structure.

## Persistence, History, and Reset Rules

Persistence is not treated as a loose implementation detail. It is divided into closed categories: Account-Core, Identity-Surfaces, Progression-Persistent, Economy-Live, Outcome-History, Participation-History, Social-Artifacts, Moderation-Artifacts, and Client-Local. In Beta and Release, account-core identity and lifecycle state persist forever. In Alpha, wipes may delete gameplay state but still must preserve handle-registry invariants such as non-reuse. Identity surfaces persist forever subject to redaction rules. Handle history persists forever, and handles can never be reused. Because account deletion does not exist, internal references remain keyed by stable player identity even when public rendering becomes redacted.

Progression-persistent data is limited to what Chapter 06 allows. Cosmetics and badges persist forever. Global Stars persist only within the current year. Outcome-history and participation-history are permanent, because the game’s legitimacy depends on the ability to represent prior seasons and yearly outcomes even after live economy state is gone. Required player-visible history includes season history, final leaderboards, Lock-In history, year history, badge visibility, and cosmetics. There is an explicit precedence rule for player-season history: if an eligible end snapshot exists, that snapshot is authoritative for the player’s final outcome representation; Lock-Ins remain visible as separate history and do not replace the end snapshot. If no eligible end snapshot exists, the latest successful Lock-In before cutoff remains authoritative for that player-season’s represented outcome.

What gets destroyed is just as important. Lock-In exit destroys that player’s live season economy state and is never recoverable. Natural season end destroys all remaining live season economy state for the season after preserving required history and completing finalization. Yearly reset wipes Global Stars and public yearly standings, rotates the year seed, increments the year sequence, and preserves cosmetics, badges, season history, and moderation state. Reset and destruction triggers are closed and include joins, Lock-In exit, season finalization, yearly reset, lifecycle wipes, beta entry wipe, profile deletion, and retention expiries or compactions. Finalization, reset, and retention work must be atomic or externally exactly once. Integrity-critical artifacts must persist forever where required for replay, fraud prevention, and proof.

## Lifecycle Phases and Release Model

Lifecycle is also globally bounded. The entire service is always in exactly one phase: Alpha, Beta, or Release. Phase flips, feature-gate flips, and ServerMode changes take effect only at the next tick boundary. Alpha is the most flexible phase. It permits invites, feature gating, and wipes, but still forbids monetization and silent competitive rule drift that would make the game untrustworthy. The Alpha to Beta transition is not a casual switch. The system must drain accepted actions, enter maintenance lockdown, execute the defined `beta_entry_wipe`, and only then switch into Beta.

Wipe types are a closed set that includes full database wipe, economy wipe, season wipe, year wipe, and beta entry wipe. Every wipe must preserve integrity chains and handle-history non-reuse. Release adds stronger promises. Determinism must be stable, season-bound rule changes may occur only at safe boundaries, outcome history must remain renderable, and there can be no retroactive mid-season outcome correction except for security or exploit fixes applied at a season boundary under audited policy. Certain constitutional rules are declared non-negotiable across the project: single lifecycle phase, auditable economy and governance operations, no staff outcome edits, deterministic acceptance and rejection, idempotent replay-safe side effects, and fixed season schedule in Beta and Release. Full account erasure still does not exist in any phase, though redaction policy may evolve for legal compliance.

## Technical References, Logging, and Replay Integrity

The technical chapter exists to make the rest of the Bible enforceable in implementation. Event families are closed and include lifecycle or configuration events, season events, player actions, trades, economy events, moderation events, wipe events, and failure or system events. Every logged event must carry immutable identifiers, time fields, tick fields, scope fields, lifecycle snapshot, and ServerMode snapshot. Season-scoped events must include season tick indices, while global-only events must not. Deterministic ordering requires both acceptance order and cross-scope serialization to be explicit in logs.

Randomness is deterministic by design. Sigil drops use a domain-separated SHA-256 based RNG stream with fixed byte encoding and integer sampling contracts. No float-based randomness is allowed. This makes every Bernoulli trial, pity bypass, and tier draw reproducible from canonical inputs. State snapshots are immutable, hash-chained, and captured at each tick commit as well as around finalization and reset boundaries. Wipe boundaries are logged explicitly and do not break integrity verification. Domain-specific logging is mandatory for trades, Sigil drop awards, and operational suppression toggles. Public exports must redact sensitive identifiers, while internal logs retain internal IDs. Economy and lifecycle logs, plus snapshot chains, are permanent. Players do not get raw-log access.

Operational safeguards assume failure can happen. Telemetry sink failure does not halt gameplay. Logs queue locally, and the service may shift into `RATE_LIMIT_ACTIONS` if needed. Incident export bundles must be immutable and include events, snapshots, lifecycle timeline, feature-gate timeline, and replay metadata. Chapter 15 also owns the closed catalogs for reason codes, feature gates, wipe types, derived client fields, DTO contracts, seed material, encoding primitives, and modifier registry. That ownership is part of documentation governance itself, because the game’s implementation depends on each closed set having a single canonical home.

## Integrated Reading of the Whole System

Taken together, the full design describes a competition that rejects most of the conveniences and ambiguities common to live-service games. It does not use permanent power ladders to retain players. It does not soften late arrivals with hidden catch-up systems. It does not let staff silently rescue bad outcomes or correct individual mistakes with private edits. It does not allow social capital to become pooled mechanical power. Instead, it builds retention through repeated meaningful decisions within a fair and legible structure. Coins enter slowly and predictably. Seasonal Stars force a trade between rank and power. Sigils offer temporary leverage under strict scarcity. Lock-In offers security at the cost of upside. Natural end offers bonus potential at the cost of exposure. Yearly reset prevents indefinite stratification. Badges and cosmetics preserve identity and prestige without creating inherited dominance.

The result is a game where trust is as much a feature as any mechanic. Players are meant to believe the schedule, the math, the rankings, the logs, and the limits on staff power. Every other chapter serves that trust. Time is fixed, action order is fixed, economic surfaces are fixed, history is preserved, moderation is bounded, and the UI is explicitly subordinate to server truth. Too Many Coins is therefore best understood not merely as an economy game, but as a tightly authored deterministic competition whose entire appeal depends on the player feeling that the machine is honest, harsh, and consistent.

## Condensed Final Definition

Too Many Coins is a deterministic four-season-overlap economy competition in which players join one live season at a time, earn Coins only through UBI, convert those Coins into Seasonal Stars, decide whether to spend those Stars for temporary Sigil-based leverage or preserve them for rank, and then choose between early Lock-In for guaranteed one-to-one conversion into Global Stars or natural season end for additional bonus potential. All live competition is governed by a one-second authoritative server tick, immutable legality windows, replay-safe math, deterministic trade and RNG rules, and strict staff non-intervention. Seasonal power never persists. Cross-season persistence is limited to current-year Global Stars, cosmetics, and prestige badges. Yearly reset wipes the numeric prestige layer, leaving only history, cosmetics, and permanent badge recognition behind.
